Introduction of the Multi Head Self Attention Mechanism

$$ \operatorname{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \operatorname{softmax}\left( \frac{Q K^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}} \right) V $$ Let’s first ignore the Q, K, V matrices. The original form of self-Attention looks like this $$ \operatorname{Softmax}(X X^\top) X $$ When a matrix is multiplied by its own transpose, it can be regarded as calculating the inner product of these vectors with other vectors. This represents the angle between two vectors and the projection of one vector onto another.

The larger the projection value is, the higher the correlation between the two vectors is. We consider that if the angle between the two vectors is 90 degrees, then these two vectors are linearly independent and have no correlation at all. Furthermore, this vector is a word vector, which is the numerical mapping of a word in a high-dimensional space. A high correlation between word vectors indicates that to some extent, when focusing on word A, word B should be given more attention. The above figure shows the result of a row vector operation. Then, the matrix $$X X^\top$$ is a square matrix. From the perspective of row vectors, it stores the results of the inner product operation between each vector and itself and other vectors. $$ \operatorname{Softmax}(z_i) = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_{c=1}^{C} e^{z_c}} $$

The significance of the Softmax operation is normalization. After the Softmax operation, the sum of these numbers becomes 1. The core of the Attention mechanism is weighted summation, and the weights are these normalized numbers. When we focus on the word “zao”, we should allocate 0.4 of the attention to itself, and the remaining 0.4 should be focused on “shang”, and 0.2 should be focused on “hao”.

Let’s take a row vector as an example. When this row vector is multiplied by a column vector, it indicates that this new row vector is the representation of the “early” word vector after being weighted and summed by the attention mechanism. The meaning is the correlation between words.

The three matrices, Q, K, and V, are essentially linear transformations of X. To enhance the model’s fitting ability, the matrices W can be trained and serve as a buffer effect. Assuming that the mean and variance of the elements in Q and K are both 0 and 1 respectively, then the mean of the elements in $$A^T = Q^T K$$ is 0 and the variance is d. When d becomes very large, the variance of the elements in A will also become very large. If the variance of the elements in A is large, then the distribution of $$\operatorname{Softmax}(A)$$ will tend to be steep, that is, the variance of the distribution is large and the distribution is concentrated in the regions with large absolute values. The distribution of $$\operatorname{Softmax}(A)$$ is related to d. Therefore, after each element in A is divided by $$\sqrt{d_k}$$, the variance becomes 1 again. This makes the “steepness” of the distribution of $$\operatorname{Softmax}(A)$$ decoupled from d, thereby ensuring that the gradient values remain stable during the training process.
